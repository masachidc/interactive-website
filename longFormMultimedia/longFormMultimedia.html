Apple's Liquid Glass

Welcome to the Future of Immersive Media

By Nathan Masachi
Listen

The Strategic Play

When Apple launched Liquid Glass, reaction critics called it a distraction, claiming it masked the company's perceived lag in AI. Others dismissed it as a minor aesthetic update.

Yet, beneath the surface, Liquid Glass represents a carefully calibrated strategic play. Apple is fundamentally a hardware-software company.

At the highest level of systems thinking, Apple's goal is to retain users within its hardware and software ecosystem as we enter the age of immersive media.

If Liquid Glass rapidly fixes its early bugs and gains mass adoption, Apple is positioning itself to become the entry point to virtual immersion.

It will have established the first bridge to immersive media, creating an environment where gestures, motion, and context become second nature to a generation of users.

Analysts project that the global immersive media market size, including augmented reality (AR), virtual reality (VR), mixed reality (MR), and related software, will reach over $5 trillion by 2035.

Apple wants to define the future of immersion.

A Little History

To understand the significance of Liquid Glass, it helps to look back at the evolution of digital media. Like humans, digital software starts in a childlike state: simple, functional, and limited.

Early interfaces were flat and two-dimensional, offering only the bare essentials for users to complete tasks. Over time, interactive elements emerged. Shadows shifted when pressed; buttons responded with haptic feedback; interfaces began to feel alive, hinting at the immersive experiences to come.

Then came glassmorphism, a design trend of the early 2020s that used translucency, blur, and layered visuals to create a sense of depth. Glassmorphism had the potential to make digital interfaces feel immersive, yet it was limited by accessibility challenges and inconsistent implementation. Many of us first encountered it in Apple and Microsoft interfaces, but few fully explored its interactive promise.

What is Liquid Glass?

Liquid Glass adds a layer of depth and refinement to glassmorphism: a transparent, responsive, and context-aware interface that reacts to gestures, movement, and device state.

Unlike static glassmorphism:

  • Interactions respond dynamically: UI elements shift, reshape, and animate according to user gestures and environment context.
  • Depth is seamless: Foreground and background merge to create a coherent spatial experience.
  • Cross-device continuity: Interactions are consistent across iPhone, iPad, Mac, and Apple Vision Pro, weaving the ecosystem into a unified platform.
  • Material intelligence: The interface senses device tilt, light, and touch pressure to simulate fluid physics and lighting behavior.

So, Why Liquid Glass?

Strategically, Apple isn't just redesigning UI. It is training a generation of users to think interactively. Apple wants its users to internalize immersive behaviors before fully stepping into AR/VR spaces.

The goal is not immediate adoption but to train users to internalize immersive behaviors. It's about familiarization with immersive digital behaviors. Remember the Apple Vision Pro? Apple's goal was to leapfrog the public into virtual and augmented reality. However, Vision Pro underperformed because it arrived too early for mainstream adoption.

Liquid Glass serves as a bridge, slowly acclimating users to the future of digital interfaces—one that will ultimately extend to smart glasses, spatial interfaces, and immersive software platforms.

What to Expect Next

The second bridge to virtual immersive worlds is smart glasses. Users are starting to adopt smart glasses. Meta's Ray-Ban line has tripled revenue year over year, proving consumer appetite for wearable, context-aware displays. Rumors suggest Apple could enter this market as early as 2027, extending Liquid Glass into three-dimensional spatial worlds.

What does this mean for users?

At this level of dynamic personalization, interacting with technology will feel less like managing a machine and more like working with something that understands you.

For companies, designers, and developers, this moment is critical. Mastering immersive, interactive media now is not a matter of band-wagoning, but rather about future-proofing skills and influence. Those who can design fluid, human-centered, cross-device experiences will dominate in a market where interfaces themselves are part of the product narrative.

From a cultural perspective, Liquid Glass exemplifies a broader shift: Mass Futurism has started! What once lived in sci-fi movies is slowly entering our lives through hardware and software as interconnected adaptive systems.

Just as smartphones taught billions to swipe, tap, and scroll, Liquid Glass and its successors will teach us to gesture, glance, and inhabit digital spaces.

Liquid Glass isn't just a new UI; it ushers in the age of immersive, interactive media. Expect many digital interfaces to be filled with glass-like interfaces in the coming decade.
